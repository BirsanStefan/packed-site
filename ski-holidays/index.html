Group ski holidays

Nothing brings people together like a group ski holiday.

Whether it's bonding with friends, taking time away with family or giving your best friend a spectacular send-off into married life, ski holidays are the best way to socialise and make memories.

The real beauty is that not all of you have to be ski-mad to enjoy the week or ski weekend. Some of the most memorable days are spent sitting on sun terraces, dancing on tables or sprawling around the fire in a cosy alpine chalet.

Group ski holidays also make for the ultimate corporate trip, giving colleagues the chance to loosen up and enjoy each other's company.

Group ski chalet holidays

If you're set on the idea of dozing off in a cosy chalet after a long day out on the slopes, you'll find no shortage of them in ski resorts across the Alps.

Ski chalet holidays are particularly ideal if you're travelling in a large group as they're often able to accommodate up to 20 people, sometimes more. Sleeping under one roof is great fun if you're a big group of friends who are keen to party or a family looking for privacy and plenty of space to stretch out.

Choose from either catered and self-catered chalets, with the former typically providing delicious meals for six days of the week. It's also common to share chalets with other groups - perfect if your group likes the idea of socialising with other skiers during your holiday.

 

Group self-catered ski holidays

Those hoping to save a little cash on their winter getaway might want to consider a self-catered ski holiday. You'll find a huge selection of apartments across all our top resorts, with some able to sleep up to ten people. A great pick for smaller groups, plus you can always book a couple of neighbouring apartments if you've got more people in tow.

Instead of spending a small fortune eating every meal out at excellent local eateries, you'll have the option of whipping up some grub in your apartment's fully-equipped kitchen. Self-catering doesn't have to be as much hassle as you'd think. Split the cooking between you and even have ingredients delivered straight to your door using Huski if you're staying in the French Alps.

On those days when no one fancies sweating over a stovetop, you'll have the flexibility to splash out and savour regional cuisine (or just a tasty burger) at a resort restaurant instead.

 

All-inclusive group ski holidays

Want a completely hassle-free group ski holiday? All-inclusive is the way to go. Not only is it the easiest option, especially if you've got fussy kids (or adultsâ€¦) in your party, but you'll also usually be able to save money by combining the cost of everything.

If you'd like a little bit more flexibility though, most chalets and hotels tend to offer half-board rates. This is ideal if you reckon you'll want to eat at least a few of your meals in the resort or on a sun-drenched terrace at the top of the mountain. Some accommodation will even offer to pack up a picnic to take with you on the pistes!

What's more, if you set us the task of booking your all inclusive group ski holiday, we'll handle everything and be your sole point of contact for the entire trip.

 

Group ski weekends

If you can only spare a few days to hit the slopes, a group ski weekend is a solid idea. It's worth noting, though, that booking weekend trips in the Alps and other European skiing hotspots requires a little extra planning. Some resorts are a bit of a trek from the nearest airport, plus you'll be a little more limited when it comes to accommodation as some chalets have a minimum nights stay policy.

Luckily, our team are pros at organising awesome ski weekends. We can offer you expert advice on the top resorts for shorter winter getaways (usually Chamonix, Morzine and Avoriaz come out top), plus help you secure ace accommodation to suit your group. It'll probably be a hotel rather than a chalet, however, you'll find they usually have the most convenient locations and a range of amazing extras - think spas, swimming pools and fitness centres.

Top tips for an awesome group ski holiday

As we've said, planning a group ski holiday isn't always a piece of cake. There are lots of things to consider and it's not unusual for things to get a bit overwhelming. Thankfully, we're brimming with ideas on how to make your group ski trip unforgettable.

Book early (if you can)

First things first, don't leave it to the last minute, especially really large groups. Start planning well in advance and you'll get what you want. The best chalets get snapped up quickly, but you'll find many come with low deposits and you can spread the cost quite nicely.

Consider name-changes

Depending on what you book, name changes are free with some providers, with the cut off date typically anywhere between 4 to 10 weeks before you travel. This is particularly useful for sports teams or larger groups where you're not certain of the uptake until nearer the time. (Incidentally, if you are uncertain of the group size and want to add people on later, a big hotel or chalet-hotel is often the easiest approach to take).

Choose the right resort

The resort you choose is critical to making the most of your trip. Not every resort is the same; some are quieter and better suited to families, while others have amazing après ski, for those keen to split their time between the bar and the mountain.

Choose the right slopes

There's also the skiing itself to consider. You don't want to venture all the way to a resort with no decent nursery slopes if your group are all complete beginners. Same goes for advanced skiers - a lack of challenging black runs or off-piste opportunities could see them quickly bored.

If you're not sure which resort to pick, we can help - send us a quick message with your requirements and we'll steer you in the right direction. If in doubt, book a mixed ability resort like one of the ones we've listed further down this page.

Alpe dâ€™Huez

Alpe d'Huez enjoys so many sunny days each season it's been given the nickname 'Island of the Sun', which makes it a great spot for tentative beginners and other-halves who need a little convincing. There are two fantastic beginner areas, the resort has a great selection of après opportunities (including its own Folie Douce), and its overall a relatively inexpensive resort. You can't go wrong.

Bansko

Bulgaria's leading ski resort is an excellent cut-price option, ideal for those willing to risk the minimum outlay on their first ski trip. The slopes are beginner friendly and the resort itself is super lively, so there's plenty to keep the group entertained when the lifts close too.

Les Arcs

One half of the enormous Paradiski area it shares with neighbouring La Plagne, Les Arcs is a resort that offers something for every kind of new skier with each of its distinct villages providing easy access to a nursery slope. Stay in the winter wonderland style resort of Arc 1950, or go for the livelier 1800 if you like to party. Les Arcs is also one of the easiest resorts in Europe to reach by train, with the Eurostar running from Kings Cross to Bourg Saint Maurice, which is a short funicular up the mountain to resort.

Méribel

Meribel is a superb resort in the centre of the enormous Trois Vallees ski area in France. There are two big nursery slopes and a huge range of cruisey greens and blues to test new skills on, plus the tuition available is excellent. Beyond the piste, the resort packs a punch in restaurant, après and non-ski activities too.

Pas de la Casa

Another cut-price alternative beyond the Alps, Pas de la Casa in Andorra is the largest ski resort in the Pyrenees. It's characterised by wide sweeping slopes through the trees and the low taxes make everything quite notably cheaper than resorts in France and Austria. There are half a dozen nursery slopes to choose from and with most of the resort above 2000m, snow cover is reliable too.

Alpe dâ€™Huez

Alpe d'Huez is another French favourite and a good all-rounder if your group is at different stages of their ski training. Nicknamed the 'Island in the Sun' due to its impressive number of sunny days annually, it's also home to the longest black run in the Alps - 16km-long Sarenne. Not at that stage yet? Alpe d'Huez is blessed with great nursery slopes and ski schools.

Les Arcs

Another great resort for groups of varying abilities, Les Arcs forms one half of the huge Paradiski area. You'll have no shortage of runs to try, plus, if you exhaust Les Arcs' pistes, you can hop on a lift to neighbouring La Plagne. The resort itself is split up into different villages which each have their own unique allures - try Les Arcs 1950 if you're holidaying with family and 1800 if you're keen to party.

Les Deux Alpes

Located smack-bang in the middle of the French Alps, Les Deux Alpes offers up an estimated 200km of prime pistes to explore. It's the second oldest ski resort in France and particularly popular with Brits thanks to its reliable snow cover, great mix of runs, chalet-style accommodation and ace après ski. It's also linked to La Grave - a well-known hotspot for off-piste skiing.

Mayrhofen

For traditional Tyrolean chalets and an abundance of pistes for all abilities, it doesn't get much better than Mayrhofen. This Austrian resort sits in a valley between two major mountains and boasts the country's steepest ski slope - the epic Harakiri. Mayrhofen village is particularly picturesque, plus you'll find plenty of places to dig into a slice of strudel or knock back a few steins.

Pas de la Casa

If you'd like to try skiing somewhere other than the Alps, Pas de la Casa is a great shout. It's located in Andorra's Grandvalira ski region and is one of only a handful of resorts in the Pyrenees. As most of the resort sits at 2000m above sea level, snow-sure slopes and high altitude skiing are major benefits. Thanks to influences from neighbouring France, Pas de la Casa also has a very decent - and affordable - après ski scene.

Val Thorens

Claiming the title of the highest ski resort in Europe, great snow coverage is pretty much a given in Val Thorens, in the Three Valleys. Its lofty location also makes it a winner with more experienced skiers, although there's a whole bunch of cruisey blues to suit novices, too. Beyond the pistes, Val Thorens is a charming and compact ski town with everything you'll need to let your hair down after dark.

Chamonix

Non-skiing activities are numerous in this French ski resort - especially if you're hoping to try more traditional alpine pastimes like husky sledding. Another popular thing to do is take the cable car all the way up to the top of the Aiguille du Midi for panoramic views of the pistes and iconic Mont Blanc. Back at the resort, why not cover some terrain on a snowmobile, soar through the sky paragliding or whizz down the Grands Montets at the end of the ski day on a sledge?

Morzine

This resort may be part of the huge Portes du Soleil ski area, but it also packs a real punch when it comes to non-skiing escapades. Fans of ice skating will find two amazing rinks within the resort, as well as a first-rate sports centre that's home to two swimming pools and a spa. You could also wrap up and explore the great outdoors on Morzine's winter hiking trails, test your mettle with ice diving or spend the evening watching the latest blockbuster at the resort's plush cinema.

Tignes

Non-skiers after a nice mix of relaxation and adrenaline-rushing exploits might find Tignes a better fit. While the skiers among you can check out the resort's incredible array of skiing, others can explore them during an ice climbing or mountaineering session instead. Tignes additionally has some sensational spas, as well as the impressive Le Lagon swimming pool if you're keen to keep up with your fitness.
